Facebook data breach: a danger for state officers

Andrea Dainese
April 04, 2021
CISO, Personal Security

A few days ago the news broke about a Facebook data breach : 553 million user records that were exposed in 2019 are now circulating freely online. The story caused an uproar because the dataset, which used to be sold for money, is now freely accessible to anyone.

Over 36 million of the records belong to Italian users and include phone numbers, Facebook IDs, dates of birth, employment details… the dataset contains executives, MPs, senators, military personnel, law enforcement officers…

Although the breach dates back to 2019, the fact that it is now public changes the threat: a new category of threat actor has effectively been added.

Threats to Individuals

Regular readers will understand why such a data leak is dangerous for people. Possession or use of the dataset is obviously illegal, but declaring that doesn’t stop attacks, stalkers, scams, unscrupulous call centres, or criminal organizations: they now have a convenient phone directory for a large portion of the Italian population.

Threats to the State

While the existence of such a dataset is not new, it’s worth reflecting on how it could be used to threaten the State. The database contains a huge number of people who perform, directly or indirectly, critical functions for the State and who have potential physical access to highly sensitive areas.

Most of these people are not remotely prepared to recognise and handle a digital assault. In a somewhat paranoid—but realistic—scenario that experts in my field consider, one could imagine a massive, coordinated attack targeting these key figures. Because it would be massive, such an attack could be unprecedented and likely highly effective.

Countermeasures

As with any data breach, once the data is out there, containment options are limited. While changing your phone number (the most sensitive exposed field here) is possible, some data—like date of birth—cannot be changed.

We must also accept that this type of criminal (misleadingly called “hacker”) is often a professional seeking to monetise skills, and sometimes even backed by intelligence services. With that in mind, we need to seriously reassess what personal data we publish online: once a datum is online, it can become public, and we must plan accordingly.

There are strategies to substantially reduce our exposure. For example, instead of giving our mobile number to every service (often touted as a way to “improve security”), we should evaluate whether the trade-off is worthwhile given the real risks—this incident shows that it may not be.

Conclusions

This story is far from over. In my view, the original impact was underestimated when the breach occurred, and it’s still being underestimated now. It’s hard to know how those who bought the data used it over the past year; it’s far easier—and far more concerning—to imagine how it will be used now that it is public.
